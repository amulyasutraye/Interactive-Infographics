Exercise and Mental Health

One study of over 1 million people showed that those who exercised three to five times per week for 30 minutes or more had a 35% lower risk for developing depression than those who didn’t exercise at all. Exercise also increases levels of endorphins – hormones associated with improved mood – which could explain why it helps improve mental health.

Meditation and Mental Wellness Apps

The global market for meditation and mental wellness apps reached over 533 million U.S. dollars in 2022. The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated the adoption of these apps, with Calm and Headspace leading the market and generating significant revenue. While adoption rates have dropped, the growing global demand for mental health support and awareness is expected to drive further development in the meditation app market.

Exercise benefits elderly sleep.

This meta-analysis provides a comprehensive review of randomised trials examining the effects of an exercise training program on sleep quality in middle-aged and older adults with sleep complaints including insomnia, depression, and poor sleep quality. These findings demonstrate that the participants did not sleep for a longer duration after participation in exercise training but they nevertheless perceived better sleep quality.

Impact of Social Media on Mental Health

According to a survey in 2017 by the Royal Society for Public Health, Britons aged 14-24 believe that Facebook, Instagram, Snapchat and Twitter have detrimental effects on their wellbeing. They also said that the platforms exacerbated anxiety and depression, deprived them of sleep, exposed them to bullying and created worries about their body image and “FOMO” (“fear of missing out”).

Workplace Mental Health Initiatives

Many companies are implementing fitness programs to promote mental well-being, recognizing the link between physical activity and reduced stress levels. A NAMI-Ipsos poll conducted in January 2025 found that a significant portion of the workforce believes mental health at work is important but lacks sufficient information and support from leadership.
